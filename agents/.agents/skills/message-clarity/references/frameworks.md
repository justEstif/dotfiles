# Message Clarity Frameworks

## PAS (Problem → Agitation → Solution)

The most reliable structure for any surface where you need someone to *want* the thing before you explain it.

```
Problem:    [Name the pain they already feel — their words, not yours]
Agitation:  [Show how it shows up in real life — makes them feel seen]
Solution:   [Your thing, now that they're ready to hear it]
```

**Landing page headline example:**
- ❌ "A communication clarity process for presentations and apps"
- ✅ "For when you know what you're trying to say — but it keeps not landing"

**Pitch opener example:**
- ❌ "This is a tool that helps you identify your audience and shape your message"
- ✅ "You've explained this a dozen times and people keep asking 'but what is it?' — this fixes that"

---

## Three Message Levels

Use the right level for the surface. Don't load all three into one message.

| Level | Question it answers | Best for |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| **Value** | What problem do you solve? What's in it for them? | Headlines, subject lines, intros |
| **Unique Value** | What can only you do? What's your onlyness? | Competitive pitches, investor decks, positioning statements |
| **Point of View** | What do you believe that others don't? | Brand narrative, thought leadership, why-us sections |

## Gap Diagnosis

When a message is failing, run this before rewriting:

1. Write out what you're currently saying (your framing)
2. Write out what the audience hears (their framing) — if you don't know, that's the problem
3. Write out what they *need* to hear to take action
4. The delta between (2) and (3) = the gap to close

Common gap types:
- **Vocabulary gap**: your words aren't in their dictionary yet
- **Problem gap**: you're describing a solution to a problem they don't think they have yet
- **Trust gap**: they understand the claim but don't believe it without proof
- **Relevance gap**: they understand it's valuable — but not for them, not now

## Surface-Specific Guidance

### Landing page
- Lead with the pain (PAS Problem) in the headline
- Subheadline does the Agitation — make them feel seen
- First CTA appears after they've felt understood, not before
- Features section is the proof, not the pitch

### Presentation
- Open with the audience's problem, not your agenda
- State your Point of View early — gives them a lens for everything that follows
- Each slide: one idea, one audience action (understand / decide / feel)

### Sensitive interpersonal message (colleague, feedback)
- Name what you *need* before you explain the context
- State your intent explicitly ("I want to resolve this without creating friction between us")
- Agitation here = acknowledging their perspective before stating yours

### Short pitch / elevator
- Problem sentence only — one sentence, their words
- What changes after — one sentence, concrete
- Skip features entirely unless asked

## Research Methods for Audience Language

When you don't know how your audience talks about their pain:

1. **Read their complaints** — support tickets, 1-star reviews of competitors, Reddit threads, Slack messages
2. **Ask the wrong question on purpose** — "what do you use for X?" often reveals what they call the problem
3. **Listen for the before-state** — "I used to spend hours on..." — the before-state is the pain
4. **Copy their exact phrases** — don't paraphrase. If they say "it keeps getting lost in the shuffle," use that phrase, not "organizational friction"
