# Message Clarity

The burden of clarity is entirely on the communicator. When someone doesn't get it, the message failed — not the audience.

**MANDATORY — READ `references/frameworks.md`** before writing any copy or structured output (landing page, pitch, slide, email). It contains the PAS framework, three-level message structure, and the gap diagnosis process.

**MANDATORY — READ `references/stakeholder-prealignment.md`** when the user needs buy-in, proposal framing, objection handling, meeting prep, stakeholder alignment, or pre-alignment before a group decision.

## Core Insight

Most communication fails because it describes **the thing** instead of **the audience's problem**. People don't recognize their own pain in feature lists.

The fix is always upstream: before shaping the message, find the conversation already happening in their head, and join it.

## Process

### Phase 1 — Identify who you're actually talking to

Ask:
- Who is the *one* person most likely to feel immediate relief when this lands?
- What were they frustrated by **the moment before** they'd reach for this?
- How do they describe that frustration — in their own words, not yours?

> Personas with name/age/job title are useless for messaging. The only persona that matters: what problem do they have, and what language do they use when talking about it?

Deliverable: a one-sentence description of the person and their moment of pain.

### Phase 2 — Find the gap

Lay both sides of the table:

| What you're saying | What they need to hear |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| What it does / is | What problem it solves for them |
| Your framing | Their framing |
| Your vocabulary | Their vocabulary |

The gap is the distance between the two columns. If the left side describes outputs and the right side is blank — that's why it's not landing.

### Phase 3 — Shape the message

Lead with their problem, not your thing. The PAS structure works for almost every surface:

1. **Problem** — name the pain they already feel
2. **Agitation** — show you understand how it manifests (makes them feel seen)
3. **Solution** — your thing, now that they're ready to hear it

Then layer in the three message levels:
- **Value** — what's in it for them? Which problem does it solve?
- **Unique value** — what can only this do? What's its "onlyness"?
- **Point of view** — what do you believe that others don't?

You don't need all three for every surface. A landing page headline needs Value. A pitch to a skeptic needs Unique Value. A colleague message needs none — just Agitation done right.

### Phase 4 — Test for recognition

Read the shaped message back through the audience's eyes. Ask:
- Does someone with that pain recognize themselves in the first sentence?
- Could they repeat back what it does after reading it once?
- Is there anything that requires inside knowledge to understand?

If any answer is no — return to Phase 2. The gap is still there.

## NEVER

- **NEVER start with features or outputs**
  **Why:** Features only resonate after the audience has already agreed they have the problem. Leading with features forces the audience to do translation work — they won't.
  **Instead:** Start with the pain. Features are the proof, not the headline.

- **NEVER use your vocabulary instead of theirs**
  **Why:** Your words are precise to you and opaque to them. Precision doesn't help if they don't recognize the category.
  **Instead:** Mine the exact phrases they use when describing their frustration. Use those words verbatim. Research: social posts, reviews, support tickets, or just ask them.

- **NEVER try to speak to everyone**
  **Why:** A message shaped for everyone is recognized by no one. "Broad" reads as vague.
  **Instead:** Name the one person in Phase 1. Write as if they're the only reader. Others who share the pain will also recognize it.

- **NEVER skip Phase 1 because you "know the audience"**
  **Why:** The gap between assumed audience and actual audience is usually where messages die.
  **Instead:** If you can't write one sentence about their moment of pain in their words, you don't know the audience yet. Interview, observe, or read what they write.

- **NEVER describe what a thing *is* when you mean to describe what it *does for someone***
  **Why:** "It's a communication clarity process" tells them nothing. "For when you know what you're trying to say but it keeps not landing" is a door they walk through.
  **Instead:** Reframe every noun into a before/after: not "what is it" but "what changes for the person who uses it."
