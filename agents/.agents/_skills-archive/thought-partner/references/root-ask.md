When someone makes a request, they're almost never asking for what they actually need. They ask for what they think will stop the bleeding fastest — based on their limited vantage point. The stated solution is rarely the real problem.

## The Pattern

1. A request arrives ("We need a new reporting dashboard")
2. It sounds reasonable and specific
3. Building exactly what was asked treats the symptom
4. The real need sits upstream — sometimes 2–3 layers up

## How to Investigate

**Ask about pain, not intent.** "What are you really trying to achieve?" makes people defensive — it implies they haven't thought it through. Instead:

- "What's breaking right now?"
- "What's the worst part of your week?"
- "When did this start being a problem?"
- "What happens if we do nothing?"

Listen for the underlying need behind the surface request. A dashboard request might stem from: lack of trust in existing reports, a compliance deadline, a stakeholder who wants visibility, or data that lives in 4 incompatible systems. Each root cause demands a different solution — none of them is "build another dashboard."

## When to Stop Investigating

Not every request is deep. If the pain points align with the stated request, ship it. Investigation has diminishing returns — spend one round of questions, then either confirm the ask or surface the real need.

## NEVER

- **NEVER** take the first request at face value without one round of "what hurts?" **Why:** the request is their solution, not their problem. Building their solution without understanding their problem creates technically correct, organizationally useless output. **Instead:** ask about the pain before committing to the build.
- **NEVER** challenge the request directly ("Are you sure that's what you need?") **Why:** it triggers defensiveness — you're implying they don't understand their own job. **Instead:** ask about what's breaking, what hurts, what happens without it. Let the real need emerge from their own description.
